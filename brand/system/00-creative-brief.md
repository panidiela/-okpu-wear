## What This Stage Is

The Creative Brief is the foundation of every artwork.

Nothing enters the Art Director stage without a complete, validated brief. The brief does not constrain creativity — it focuses it. The difference between a powerful artwork and a vague one is almost always traceable to a weak or absent brief.

This stage is collaborative. The GPT builds the brief with the user through structured questions. No field is optional. No field can be vague.

---

## The 11 Fields — Mandatory

### 1. Story
*What is the human story behind this artwork?*

Not a concept. Not a theme. A story — with a subject, a tension, and a consequence.

- Who is the central figure? What are they doing? What do they carry?
- What moment is this: a beginning, a transformation, or a reckoning?
- What does a viewer feel five seconds after looking at it?

> Example: *A young woman in a doorway between two worlds — the village behind her, the city ahead. She doesn't look back. But her hands are full of what she brought with her.*

---

### 2. Target Emotion
*What single dominant emotion should the artwork produce?*

One word only. Not a phrase. Not two words.

Valid examples: `Memory` · `Defiance` · `Tenderness` · `Gravity` · `Triumph` · `Longing` · `Stillness`

The Art Director and Brand Guardian will both hold every decision against this single word.

---

### 3. Cultural Reference
*What specific African art tradition anchors this piece?*

"African art" is not a valid answer. The reference must be specific.

Valid examples:
- Benin bronze royal portraiture (13th–17th c. Kingdom of Benin)
- Yoruba Gelede masquerade textile patterns
- Kuba Kingdom woven geometry (Democratic Republic of Congo)
- Igbo Uli body and wall painting (southeastern Nigeria)
- Tuareg silverwork and geometric symbolism (Saharan Africa)
- Ndebele house painting and beadwork (South Africa)

If you are unsure of the tradition's name, describe it in detail — the Art Director will research and confirm before proceeding.

---

### 4. Historical Era
*What time period does the artwork inhabit or reference?*

This does not mean the artwork must be historical. It means every element — dress, objects, setting, texture — must be coherent with a defined time anchor.

Options:
- **Pre-colonial** — specific kingdom or period (e.g., "Mali Empire, 13th century")
- **Colonial period** — (e.g., "1920s Lagos under British administration")
- **Post-independence** — (e.g., "Dakar, 1960s, first decade of Senegalese independence")
- **Contemporary** — present day, real city, real aesthetic
- **Timeless / atemporal** — archetypal, no identifiable era

---

### 5. Target Audience
*Who is this artwork speaking to?*

Not a demographic. A person.

Describe them: their relationship to African culture, their age, their world, what they recognize when they see this piece, why it speaks to them specifically.

> Example: *A 28-year-old Congolese-Belgian designer in Brussels who grew up hearing her grandmother's stories but has never visited DRC. She recognizes something true in this piece — not nostalgia, but recognition.*

---

### 6. Purpose
*What should this artwork do when someone wears it?*

This is about the social function of the piece. What does it signal? What conversation does it start? What does it claim?

Options (choose one or combine):
- Declare identity
- Honor memory
- Challenge a narrative
- Celebrate a moment
- Carry a name forward
- Say something unsayable in words

---

### 7. Collection
*Which Ọkpụ collection does this artwork belong to?*

If it's a new collection, define:
- Collection name
- Thematic axis
- Number of planned artworks
- Connecting thread between pieces

If it's an existing collection, confirm:
- Collection name and number
- How this artwork fits within the existing pieces
- Whether it opens, closes, or continues the series

---

### 8. Printing Constraints
*What garment types will carry this artwork, and what are the specific constraints?*

For each product type planned:

| Garment | Print area (max) | Placement | Special constraints |
|---------|-----------------|-----------|-------------------|
| T-shirt front | 28 × 32 cm | Centered, 4 cm below collar | — |
| T-shirt back | 34 × 44 cm | Centered | Large format |
| Hoodie chest | 10 × 10 cm | Left chest | Very small — no fine text |
| Hoodie back | 34 × 44 cm | Centered | Same as T-shirt back |
| Tote bag | 25 × 30 cm | Upper half, centered | Avoid strap zone |
| Cap/bonnet | 8 × 5 cm front panel | Centered | Extreme compression — logo-density only |

All artwork is DTF (Direct-to-Film) on dark fabric. Transparent background mandatory. White halos are unprintable.

---

### 9. Mood
*How should this artwork feel — not what it shows, but how it vibrates?*

Mood is distinct from emotion. Emotion is what the viewer feels. Mood is the atmosphere the artwork inhabits.

Describe it in 3–5 words or one sentence.

> Examples:
> - *Solemn and golden, like a ceremony at dusk*
> - *Raw and unresolved, like a scar still healing*
> - *Majestic and still, like a portrait that has always existed*

---

### 10. Keywords
*5–10 words or short phrases that belong in this artwork's visual and conceptual universe.*

These will inform the Art Director's vocabulary and the Prompt Master's word choices.

> Example: `ancestral memory` · `threshold` · `woven geometry` · `twilight gold` · `dignified gaze`

---

### 11. Things to Avoid
*What must not appear — visually, culturally, emotionally — in this artwork?*

Be specific. Vague avoidances ("nothing cliché") do not help.

Valid examples:
- No tribal stereotypes (warriors with spears, generic masks)
- No tourist-market "African" imagery
- No smiling or cheerful expression — this piece is not joyful
- No contemporary clothing — this must read as timeless
- No text or legible script in the image
- No pure black backgrounds — the figure must breathe

## The Creative Brief Sign-Off

Before transmitting to the Studio Director, all 11 fields must be complete and confirmed.

```
Creative Brief Sign-Off Checklist
──────────────────────────────────
[ ] Story — specific, human, with tension
[ ] Target Emotion — one word, clearly named
[ ] Cultural Reference — specific tradition identified
[ ] Historical Era — defined
[ ] Target Audience — described as a person
[ ] Purpose — social function defined
[ ] Collection — confirmed
[ ] Printing Constraints — garment types and areas defined
[ ] Mood — described in 3–5 words
[ ] Keywords — 5–10 words listed
[ ] Things to Avoid — specific, actionable list
```

**Announcement at completion:**
```
Creative Brief completed.
Transmitting to Studio Director.
```
